HP35s¶

modexp¶

Description¶

This program calculates the modulus of a number raised to a large power. The formula looks like this:

\[{modexp} = n^p\ \bmod m\]
Usage¶

GTO A001 n R/S p R/S m R/S

Program Listing¶

R1.25cm L2.5cm L10.0cm

& LBL A &start of program
& STO N &store number to the raised to the power P
& STOP &wait for user R/S
& STO P &store exponent
& STOP &wait for user R/S
& STO M &store modulus
& 1 &initialize product …
& STO R &… and save in memory
& RCL N &recall base …
& RCL R &recall product …
& \times &… and multiply the two
& RCL M &recall the modulus …
& RMDR &… and apply it
& STO R &save the new product
& DSE P &decrement exponent …
& GTO A009 &… and loop back if not finished.
& RCL R &pull the product from memory
& RTN &we are done!

Example¶

In the following example we calculate \(\mathbf{5^{101} \bmod 31}\) using the following steps: GTO A001 0.00000 5 R/S 5.00000 101 R/S 101.00000 31 R/S 25.00000

Comments¶

The HP35s is not known for it’s lightning speed. The above example will take about 12 seconds to run.

atan2¶

Description¶

This program calculates \({\rm atan2}(\frac{y}{x})\). Result is in the range \(-180\) to \(+180\).

Usage¶

GTO Z001 x R/S y R/S

Program Listing¶

R1.25cm L2.5cm L10.0cm

& LBL Z &Start of program.
& STO X &Store \(x\) in X.
& STOP &Wait for user R/S.
& STO Y &Store \(y\) in Y.
& RCL Y &Recall Y. Note: this is also the entry point for subroutine.
& RCL X &Recall X.
& \div &Take ratio of rise over run (\(\frac{y}{x}\)).
& ATAN &Calculate \(\arctan(\frac{y}{x})\).
& STO R &Save as an interim result in R.
& RCL X &Test sign of X.
& x > 0? &Is \(x\) positive?
& GTO Z027 &If so then go to end of program.
& RCL Y &Recall Y …
& SGN &Calculate its sign …
& 45 &
& \times &then multiply it by 45.
& RCL X &Get X value.
& x = 0? &Is it equal to zero?
& RTN &If so then return the value of the stack (\(\pm\)45)
& 180 &Setup offset depending on sign of \(y\).
& STO -R &Initially subtract 180— we do this at a minimum.
& RCL Y &Get Y value.
& x < 0? &Is it negative?
& GTO Z027 &If yes, then we are done since we already subtracted 180.
& 360 &If \(y\) is positive then we have to add 360…
& STO +R &… for a total addition of 180.
& RCL R &Get the angle.
& RTN &Return to calling function.

Example¶

In the following example we calculate \({\rm atan2}(\frac{+1.5}{-1.0})\) using the following steps: GTO Z001 0.00000 1.5 1.5 R/S 1.50000 -1.0 -1.0 R/S -33.69007

Comments¶

Users have to be careful about a couple of things:

  1. Angles are calculated in degrees. Confirm calculator setting before using this function.

  2. User is responsible for ensuring that \(x\) and \(y\) are never both zero.

Example¶

Calculate the actual VSWR of an antenna where the measured VSWR is 1:1.13 and cable loss is 4.7dB: GTO V001 0.00000 1.13 ```` R/S 1.13000 4.7 ```` R/S 1.4394

How this program works¶

\(VSWR\) is the ratio of the sum and difference of forward and reflected voltages:

\[VSWR = {{V_f+V_r}\over{V_f-V_r}}\]

Since power is proportional to the square of the voltage, (1) can be expressed in terms of power

\[VSWR = {{\sqrt{P_f}+\sqrt{P_r}}\over{\sqrt{P_f}-\sqrt{P_r}}}\]

By setting \(P_f\) equal to \(1\) and rearranging (2) we can solve for \(P_r\)

\[P_r = \left({{VSWR-1}\over{VSWR+1}}\right)^{2}\]

Cable loss is normally expressed in decibels but for our purposes we can simply express it as a factor of less than 1 where \(P_a\) is power at the antenna and \(P_t\) is power from the transmitter.

\[L = {{P_a }\over{ P_t}}\]

Taking into account the cable loss and measured forward and reverse power we can calculate VSWR at the antenna

\[VSWR = {{\sqrt{P_f \times L}+\sqrt{P_r \div L}}\over{\sqrt{P_f \times L}-\sqrt{P_r \div L}}}\]

Again we set \(P_f\) to \(1\) so a simplied version of (5) becomes the actual VSWR at the antenna

\[VSWR = {{\sqrt{L}+\sqrt{P_r \div L}}\over{\sqrt{L}-\sqrt{P_r \div L}}}\]

Description¶
